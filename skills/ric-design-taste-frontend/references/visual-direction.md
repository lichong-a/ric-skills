# Visual Direction

Use this reference when selecting or reviewing hierarchy, layout, typography, materiality, copy, and visual distinctiveness.

## Brief Signals

Read these together:

- Surface: landing page, portfolio, editorial page, campaign, product overview, or redesign.
- Audience: buyer, evaluator, consumer, creator, developer, applicant, or existing customer.
- Desired action: understand, trust, compare, sign up, purchase, contact, or explore.
- Brand evidence: existing logo, type, colors, photography, product UI, and voice.
- Constraints: accessibility, regulation, performance, localization, content volume, and device mix.

## Direction Dials

Use three relative dials. Record the reason for each value.

| Dial | Low | High |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Expression | Quiet, conventional, trust-first | Experimental, memorable, immersive |
| Motion | Static or state-only | Narrative or spatial choreography |
| Density | Spacious and focused | Rich, layered, information-dense |

These dials are decisions, not automatic prescriptions. A highly expressive direction may still use little motion. A dense page may still have strong hierarchy.

## Hierarchy And Rhythm

- Make the first viewport identify the product, brand, person, place, or offer.
- Keep the primary action visually and semantically clear.
- Let each section have one dominant purpose.
- Vary section composition when the content changes; repeat structures when repetition improves comprehension.
- Use spacing, alignment, contrast, and scale before adding containers.
- Keep important content visible at realistic desktop and mobile viewports.

## Typography

- Choose type for audience, voice, language coverage, and performance.
- Define roles for display, heading, body, label, data, and caption text.
- Keep line lengths readable and prevent display text from clipping.
- Use emphasis selectively; do not make every section shout.
- Verify Chinese and multilingual glyph coverage when relevant.

## Color And Material

- Define semantic roles rather than a list of attractive colors.
- Ensure text, controls, focus, errors, and states remain legible.
- Use gradients, glass, grain, glow, bevel, or texture only when the brand direction supports them.
- Make material choices consistent enough to read as one system, but allow intentional contrast.
- Check light and dark themes independently when both are required.

## Copy And Product Specificity

- Use real product concepts, actions, constraints, and evidence.
- Keep CTA labels specific to the action.
- Remove generic claims that could describe any competitor.
- Avoid invented metrics, testimonials, customers, and guarantees.
- Treat generated image text as concept-only until verified.

## Anti-Template Review

Ask:

1. Could this page be relabeled for an unrelated product with only copy changes?
2. Does the hero reveal the actual subject or a generic visual effect?
3. Does every visual pattern have a task-specific reason?
4. Are repeated sections helping comparison or merely filling the page?
5. Does the page have a recognizable point of view without harming usability?

If the answer exposes genericity, change hierarchy, product framing, narrative, or interaction model before polishing decoration.

## Redesign Preservation

Before redesigning, capture:

- Existing brand and navigation behavior.
- Content and SEO requirements.
- Working components and user flows.
- Known accessibility and performance characteristics.
- Baseline screenshots at target viewports.

Do not silently replace these. Record intentional changes and validate preserved behavior.
